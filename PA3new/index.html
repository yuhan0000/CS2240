The History.

Keyboards to Switches

The Emergence

The first emergence of a mechanical keyboard was in 1714 created by Henry Mill. It was a Mechanical Typewriter.

The Hobby

For the longest time it was considered to be an expensive hobby, it still is, but there are more options to choose from today. It was only recently where Mechanical Keyboards became more accessible to the consumer public and more affordable options emerged. Every detail, every part, every material used is important in creating a custom keyboard that suits your liking.





Modernizatoin

In 1869, Thomas Edison invented the first electronic typewriter named Universal Stock Ticker. Fastforward to 1970s where mechanical keyboards where refined and in 1986, IBM release the Model M keyboard which was the game changing, iconic mechanical keyboard which used individual mechanical switches, and highlighted the QWERTY layout which is still used and is the standard of keyboards till this day.

SWITCHES

FOUR MAIN SWITCH DIFFERENCES

BLUE SWITCH

Blue provides tactile feedback and has a loud and clear click sound. These switches make sure, you recognize hitting the actuation point and therefore allow for really fast typing.

RED SWITCH

Red Switch is a linear switch that has no tactile feedback, lighter actuation force which means lesser effort to trigger the switch. Good for gaming.

BROWN SWITCH

Brown is one of the most popular switch types right now. The switch represents quite a nice hybrid between gaming and typing. The switch provides a tactile feedback, but does not generate a sound like the Blue.

BLACK SWITCH

Black is a linear switch, so it does not provide any feedback when hitting the actuation point and it just generates noise when bottoming out. With 60 cN, the actuation force is quite high. As a consequence, the likelihood of accidental presses might get reduced. On the other hand, it can be quite tiresome to traverse the entire switch at every key press.

Buckling Spring Switch

colloquially known as BS switches, were invented by Richard Hunter Harris and were later patented by IBM in 1977. The name was derived from how the switch itself operated. Simply put, pressing on the key exerts force down on a spring mechanism underneath until it buckles to the side and triggers a switch. These switch was used for the Model F Keyboard and the well known Model M keyboard of IBM.

Cherry Switches

Cherry had initially been part of the automobile industry, producing switches for various machinery. However, they started making keyboards in 1967, and are still doing so to this day, lending credence to their claim of being the oldest keyboard manufacturers still in business. When they received the patent for their mechanical keyboard switches in 1984, Cherry eventually introduced the switch that put their name on the market; the Mechanical X-Point Switches. Also known as the MX switches for short.

The earliest switch, the Cherry MX Black, is still made today. That said, there are several more variations to the MX Switches at present. Cherry switches are the standard on the market and are the most commonly used in mechanical keyboards.

Alp Switches

The keyboard was used on the first computer at UCLA. Made from Alps Electric, and first introduced in 1983. It was one of the first commonly used compact-design key switches available. The several variations of switches had tactile and linear switches, and all designs had a high number of parts, and their sense method involved metal contact. Each switch had 10-11 parts, leading to some calling them complicated Alps.

The earliest Alps switches didn’t have logos, but Alps Electric had their logo molded into the base of the switch. Alps switches had been used exclusively with Alps keyboards. While Alps are no longer producing keyboard switches, having stopped in 2012, they had been produced en masse for nearly twenty years.

Modern Switches

Cherry, Gateron, Kailh, Logitech, Razer, Steelseries

Ever since Cherry's patent expired in 2014, the rise of other switch brands with somewhat similar design but with different feel and quality has been available to the public. With other brands making their own designs, they are able to cater to different needs of keyboard enthusiasts.

Chinese Switches

Akko, Gazzew, Feker, Outemu

With the rise of mechanical swithces and its wide variety of options, it comes with a hefty pricetag. Chinese switch companies has offered high quality switches for almost half the price of the other reputable brands. Offering almost the same feel, performance, and modability it's obvious that these brands are making a statement in the mechanical keyboard community and are also being chosen over some other brands.

The Affordable Ones.

Switches of quality, for cheap.

AKKO CS SWITCH

AKKO CS Switch

The newest in the market that offers the same quality and feel with expensive switches, for only 1/4 of the price. It offers the Thocky Tactile feel that enthusiasts are after.

FEKER

FEKER

Known to perfectly copy the Holy Panda Switches, they where able to also make switches that offer the premium feel and thock that enthusiasts are after. With the Thocky tactile feel, this is the best alternative for the Holy Panda Switches for a fraction of the cost.

OUTEMU

OUTEMU

Known to be the cheapest of them all, Outemu doesnt offer the premium feel, but they offer the cheapest option for someone who wants to get into the hobby. It may not be premium but the switches offer modability and with enough mods, you can get a decent sounding and feeling switch. Most cheap mechanical keyboards use outemu due to its abundancy and affordability.

GAZZEW

GAZZEW

Known to be the most expensive of the cheap category, the higher price equates for its premuim build.
